Loaf guide

Ilya's Sourdough Guide

Everything you need to bake a great sourdough loaf — from your first levain to pulling a golden crust from the oven.

Before you begin

Equipment you need

  • Digital kitchen scales (1 g accuracy)
  • Kitchen bowl, minimum 3 litre capacity
  • Dough whisk or sturdy wooden spoon
  • A chopstick
  • Bowl scraper (semi-circular)
  • Clean glass or plastic jar (min 600 ml) — your levain jar
  • Razor blade (lame) or sharp knife
  • A banneton, bowl or basket · lint-free cloth if banneton is not cloth-lined
  • A dutch oven, baking stone with lid, or other suitable baking dish
  • Bakers couche (proofing cloth) or a clean floured pillow case
  • Large flat baking tray
  • Baking paper · Cling wrap or shower cap · Oven-proof gloves · Cooling rack
Nice to have

In order of importance: a dough whisk, bowl scraper, banneton (loaves) or couche (baguettes), and a dutch oven. I also have accurate digital tea scales (0.01 g accuracy) for salt. All can be bought on Amazon for well under $100 in total.

A bit of theory

Baker's percentages

Most ingredients are expressed as a percentage of the total flour in the bread. Hydration refers to water as a percentage of total flour. Salt is usually 2% and mix-ins around 15%.

About hydration

Your starter is kept at 100% hydration — always feed with equal parts flour and water. This recipe is at 70% hydration: a good balance, not too sticky and reasonably easy to work.

A bit of science

Bread flour contains starch and proteins. Water activates the proteins into gluten — an elastic network that traps gas. Yeasts in the starter produce CO₂ (rise), bacteria produce lactic acid (sour taste and preservation).

Ingredients

Flour

I recommend Wallaby brand white bread flour — sold in 5 kg bags in most supermarkets (~$14). The higher protein content compared to plain flour gives a better crumb structure.

Step 1 · ~24 hours before baking

The pre-ferment

Combine 20 g sourdough starter with 40 g flour and 40 g water in your levain jar. Mix well, cover loosely, and leave at room temperature. This produces roughly 83 g of levain by mixing time.

After mixing the levain, feed your starter with equal parts flour and water. 20 g each gives you 60 g of starter — enough for several weeks in the fridge.

Starter care

Keep starter in a clean glass jar in the fridge. Whenever you bake, replace what you use with equal parts flour and water. Feed weekly if baking less than once a week. For absences over two weeks, make a stiff dry levain (twice as much flour as water) — keeps for months.

Step 2 · ~12–14 hours before baking

Mixing the dough

Add ingredients to your bowl in this order:

  1. 1Measure out 83 g of levain into your mixing bowl
  2. 2Add 346 g water
  3. 3Add 11 g salt
  4. 4Mix for 15 seconds using a chopstick, dough whisk or fork
  5. 5Add 511 g flour
  6. 6Mix with dough whisk or wooden spoon for 45–60 seconds
  7. 7Wet your hands and bowl scraper. Cut through any dry patches and combine into a homogenous mass
  8. 8Cover with food wrap or towel and leave on benchtop
No kneading required

Gluten structures develop on their own. Keep the dough covered throughout to prevent drying out.

Optional — autolyse

Mix flour and water and rest 30–60 minutes before adding levain and salt. Improves texture and reduces mixing time.

Step 3 · During the 1.5–2 hours after mixing

Bulk fermentation

Adding mix-ins

If adding olives, toasted walnuts or other mix-ins, incorporate them during the second stretch and fold. This works for both loaves and baguettes.

Stretch and fold

Watch: Stretch & fold technique

Once every 30–45 minutes, for a total of 3 sets:

  1. 1Wet your hands and bowl scraper
  2. 2Pinch the side of the dough, stretch away from the mass, fold to the opposite side
  3. 3Rotate bowl 90° and repeat — 4 folds in total
  4. 4Cover the bowl again

Timing is flexible. You can refrigerate the bowl for 8–10 hours and take it out ~2 hours before shaping.

Step 4 · After last stretch and fold

Shaping the dough

Leave covered for another 1.5–2 hours. The dough should have risen ~50–75% and feel airy. Lightly flour a clean benchtop, your hands and the scraper.

Divide and pre-shape

Watch: Divide & pre-shape

Gently take a quarter of the dough, stretch it slightly and fold to the opposite side. Repeat three more times to form a rough ball. Turn so the floured side is on top. Dust lightly and rest 15–20 minutes.

Final shape

Watch: Final shaping
  1. 1Flour hands and scraper. Dust banneton with rice flour
  2. 2Flip dough over. Fold two opposite sides into the middle to create a rectangular shape
  3. 3Roll up like rolling carpet to create a cylinder
  4. 4Drag the cylinder across the benchtop to build surface tension
  5. 5Dust with rice flour and flip into banneton. Cover and refrigerate 4–12 hours
Shaping tips

Use the benchtop's friction to build tension in the outer skin. Think sticky side (bottom of banneton) and smooth side (becomes the top of the loaf). You can also stitch the sticky side together in the banneton for extra tension.

Step 5 · Baking day

Baking the loaf

  1. 1At least 30 minutes before baking, place baking dish and lid in oven (lid open) and preheat to maximum (up to 250°C)
  2. 25 minutes before baking, take banneton from fridge. Cut baking paper to size for a cradle
  3. 3Sprinkle dough with rice flour. Place paper cradle over banneton, flip, remove banneton
  4. 4Wet razor blade. Score — roughly 5 mm deep at 45° to the dough, parallel to the long axis
  5. 5Lower loaf into hot baking dish using paper as a cradle. Close lid
  6. 6Bake at maximum heat for 20 minutes with lid on
  7. 7Remove lid (and paper if possible). Reduce to 220°C. Bake further 20–25 minutes until deep golden
  8. 8Cool on rack for at least 1 hour before slicing
Why the lid matters

Steam is the key — the loaf rises before the crust sets. The lid traps steam from the dough itself. Don't cut for at least an hour — escaping steam makes the crumb gummy.

Good luck! 🍞

Step 4 · After last stretch and fold

Shaping baguettes

Leave covered 1.5–2 hours after the last fold. Dough should have risen ~50–75%. Lightly flour a clean benchtop, your hands and the scraper.

Divide and pre-shape

Watch: Divide & pre-shape

Use your scraper (and scales for accuracy) to divide the dough into equal portions. Shape each into a rough cylinder by folding the sides in. Dust with flour and rest 15–20 minutes.

Final shape

  1. 1Dust couche generously with rice flour or semolina. Place on a large tray and create wave-like folds — baguettes will proof in these
  2. 2Flip cylinder over. Flatten into a rectangle. Fold the long side to two-thirds and seal with flat hand. Fold remaining third to make a cigar shape
  3. 3Flour hands well. From the middle, roll baguette back and forth, increasing pressure toward the ends to elongate
  4. 4Dust generously with semolina or rice flour and place in a fold of the couche
  5. 5Cover with kitchen towel and refrigerate 4–12 hours. Avoid very long proofs — baguettes are more delicate than loaves
Shaping tips

Same principle as loaves — use friction to build tension. Baguettes are more forgiving in shape but less forgiving with over-proofing.

Step 5 · Baking day

Baking baguettes

  1. 1Preheat oven to maximum (up to 250°C) at least 30 minutes before baking. Place a heavy pan (cast iron ideal) on the oven floor
  2. 25 minutes before baking, take couche from fridge
  3. 3Cover tray with baking paper, sprinkle with rice flour or semolina
  4. 4Carefully transfer baguettes to tray — maximum 3 per standard tray
  5. 5Score each baguette — roughly 5 mm deep at an almost horizontal angle, slightly off-centre, overlapping by about a third
  6. 6Just before loading, pour boiling water over a towel in the hot pan to create steam. Optionally mist baguettes lightly with water
  7. 7Bake at maximum heat with steam for at least 12 minutes
  8. 8Vent or remove steam. Reduce to 220°C and bake a further 10–15 minutes until deep golden
  9. 9Cool on rack for at least 40 minutes before cutting
Steam is essential for baguettes

Without steam the crust sets too early and you lose the characteristic open crumb and thin crackly crust. Keep steam going for the first 12–15 minutes.

Good luck! 🥖
